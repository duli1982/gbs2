AI Hiring Governance

Practical rules for using AI in recruiting: what's allowed, what's not, how to avoid bias, and safe prompt templates you can copy today.

Jump to Safe Prompt Pack

Note: this page is guidance for responsible use. Always follow client, local, and company policies.

What you may do

  • Draft job descriptions, outreach, interview guides, and scorecards.
  • Summarize evidence from a resume against a JD (no "fit score").
  • Identify gaps/ambiguities and propose questions to validate.
  • Create structured evaluation criteria and rubrics for humans.

What you may not do

  • Use AI as an automated decision-maker (screen/shortlist/reject) without human review.
  • Infer protected characteristics (age, ethnicity, health, pregnancy, religion, etc.).
  • Store or share candidate PII in tools that are not approved for that data.
  • Use "cultural fit" scoring; focus on job-related criteria only.

No automated decisioning

Treat AI outputs as suggestions. Final decisions must be made by trained recruiters/hiring managers with documented job-related evidence and consistent criteria.

Candidate data rules (quick checklist)

Minimize
  • Use the minimum data needed to complete the task.
  • Redact contact details and identifiers if possible.
Control
  • Use only approved tools for candidate content.
  • Do not paste candidate data into public LLMs.
Document
  • Keep a clear rationale tied to the job requirements.
  • Record what AI was used for (drafting, summarizing, etc.).
Retain safely
  • Do not store AI outputs containing PII outside approved systems.
  • Follow retention rules (client + legal + company).

Bias & adverse-impact guardrails

  • Use consistent criteria for everyone; avoid "vibes" evaluation.
  • Do not use proxies for protected characteristics (school prestige, gaps, location) as hard filters.
  • Ask AI to propose verification questions, not conclusions.
  • Prefer structured interviews and job-related work samples.

Safe Prompt Pack (copy-ready)

These prompts avoid automated decisioning and "fit scores". They focus on evidence, gaps, and structured interviewing.

1) Evidence-based resume summary (no scoring) 
Act as a recruiter.

Task: Summarize the evidence in this resume against the job requirements, without giving a single "fit score" or ranking.

Rules:
- Use only job-related information in the resume; do not infer protected characteristics.
- Separate "Evidence" from "Assumptions".
- If information is missing or unclear, list questions to validate.
- Suggest structured interview questions mapped to each key requirement.

Inputs:
Job requirements: [PASTE JD REQUIREMENTS]
Resume: [PASTE RESUME TEXT]

Output format:
- Requirements mapping table (Requirement | Evidence from resume | Missing/unclear | Validation question)
- Risks/flags (job-related only)
- Suggested interview questions (by competency)
2) Structured interview kit + scorecard (human-led) 
Act as an interview designer for a structured hiring process.

Create a structured interview kit for: [ROLE]

Rules:
- No automated decisioning: humans assess responses using the rubric you create.
- Questions must be job-related and non-discriminatory.
- Include "strong / acceptable / weak" indicators for each question.

Inputs:
Job requirements: [PASTE JD REQUIREMENTS]

Output format:
1) Interview plan (stages + who interviews + purpose)
2) Question bank (Competency | Question | What good looks like | Red flags)
3) Scorecard rubric (Competency | Rating anchors | Notes)
3) Outreach message (privacy-safe) 
Write a concise, professional outreach message for [ROLE].

Rules:
- Do not include sensitive personal data.
- Focus on role value proposition and clear next step.
- Offer an opt-out.

Inputs:
Company/context: [CONTEXT]
Candidate public profile highlights: [BULLETS]

Output format:
Subject line options (3)
Message (120-180 words)
Follow-up (1 short message)
